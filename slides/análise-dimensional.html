Análise Dimensional

  • A toda grandeza medida ou calculada está associada uma dimensão.
  • Análise Dimensional é uma técnica utilizada para verificar a consistência de equações físicas ou para derivar relações entre grandezas físicas.
  • Ela se baseia nas dimensões fundamentais (comprimento, massa e tempo) das grandezas envolvidas.
  • As dimensões fundamentais são representadas por letras maiúsculas:
    • L: comprimento
    • M: massa
    • T: tempo
  • As unidades nas quais as grandezas são expressas não afetam a dimensão das grandezas.
  • Todas as equações devem ser dimensionalmente consistentes; ou seja, as dimensões nos dois lados de uma equação devem ser as mesmas.

Velocity and Speed

  • Average Velocity ($\bar{v}$): Rate of change of position. Vector.
    $\bar{v} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{x_f - x_i}{t_f - t_i}$
  • Average Speed ($\bar{s}$): Total distance traveled divided by time. Scalar.
    $\bar{s} = \frac{\text{Total Distance}}{\Delta t}$
  • Instantaneous Velocity ($v$): Velocity at a specific moment in time. The limit of average velocity as $\Delta t \to 0$. Vector.
    $v = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{dx}{dt}$
  • Instantaneous Speed: Magnitude of instantaneous velocity. $|v|$. Scalar.

Acceleration

  • Average Acceleration ($\bar{a}$): Rate of change of velocity. Vector.
    $\bar{a} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v_f - v_i}{t_f - t_i}$
  • Instantaneous Acceleration ($a$): Acceleration at a specific moment. The limit of average acceleration as $\Delta t \to 0$. Vector.
    $a = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}$
  • Units: meters per second squared (m/s²).
  • Important: Acceleration direction indicates how velocity is *changing*, not necessarily the direction of motion.
    • Positive $a$: Velocity becomes more positive (or less negative).
    • Negative $a$: Velocity becomes more negative (or less positive).
    • Speeding up: $v$ and $a$ have the same sign.
    • Slowing down: $v$ and $a$ have opposite signs.

Graphical Analysis of Motion

Graphs provide powerful visual insights into motion.

  • Position vs. Time ($x-t$) graph
  • Velocity vs. Time ($v-t$) graph
  • Acceleration vs. Time ($a-t$) graph

Understanding the relationships between these graphs is key!

Position vs. Time ($x-t$) Graph

  • Plots position ($y$-axis) against time ($x$-axis).
  • Slope: The slope of the $x-t$ graph gives the instantaneous velocity ($v = dx/dt$).
    • Positive slope: Positive velocity (moving in + direction).
    • Negative slope: Negative velocity (moving in - direction).
    • Zero slope: Zero velocity (at rest).
  • Curvature: Relates to acceleration.
    • Concave up (like a smile): Positive acceleration.
    • Concave down (like a frown): Negative acceleration.
    • Straight line: Constant velocity (zero acceleration).

Velocity vs. Time ($v-t$) Graph

  • Plots velocity ($y$-axis) against time ($x$-axis).
  • Slope: The slope of the $v-t$ graph gives the instantaneous acceleration ($a = dv/dt$).
  • Area Under the Curve: The area between the $v-t$ curve and the time axis represents the displacement ($\Delta x = \int v dt$).
    • Area above t-axis: Positive displacement.
    • Area below t-axis: Negative displacement.
  • Horizontal line: Constant velocity ($a=0$).
  • Straight line with non-zero slope: Constant acceleration.

Acceleration vs. Time ($a-t$) Graph

  • Plots acceleration ($y$-axis) against time ($x$-axis).
  • Often simpler in introductory physics, especially with constant acceleration (horizontal line).
  • Area Under the Curve: The area between the $a-t$ curve and the time axis represents the change in velocity ($\Delta v = \int a dt$).

Motion with Constant Acceleration

A very common and important special case!

If acceleration $a$ is constant, we can derive specific equations relating $x, v, a,$ and $t$.

Let $t_i = 0$, $x_i = x_0$, $v_i = v_0$, $x_f = x$, $v_f = v$, $t_f = t$.

These are the "Kinematic Equations".

The Kinematic Equations (for constant $a$)

  1. Velocity as a function of time:
    $v = v_0 + at$
    (Derived from $a = \Delta v / \Delta t$)
  2. Position as a function of time:
    $\Delta x = x - x_0 = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2} a t^2$
    (Derived by integrating $v(t)$ or using area of $v-t$ graph)

The Kinematic Equations (cont.)

  1. Velocity as a function of position:
    $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2 a \Delta x$
    (Derived by eliminating $t$ between eq. 1 and 2)
  2. (Optional but useful) Position using average velocity:
    $\Delta x = \frac{1}{2} (v_0 + v) t$
    (Valid only for constant $a$)

Choose the equation that fits your knowns and unknowns!

Problem-Solving Strategy

  1. Read Carefully: Understand the situation.
  2. Draw a Diagram: Sketch the motion, label axes, indicate initial/final points. Choose an origin and positive direction.
  3. List Knowns & Unknowns: Identify given values ($x_0, v_0, a, t, x, v$) and what you need to find. Ensure units are consistent!
  4. Choose Equation(s): Select the kinematic equation(s) that contain your knowns and the desired unknown.
  5. Solve Algebraically: Rearrange the equation to solve for the unknown variable *before* plugging in numbers.
  6. Calculate: Substitute numerical values with units.
  7. Check Answer: Does the sign make sense? Is the magnitude reasonable? Do the units match?

Example 1: Accelerating Car

A car starts from rest and accelerates uniformly at $3.0 \text{ m/s}^2$ for $5.0 \text{ s}$.

(a) What is its final velocity?

(b) How far does it travel in this time?

Knowns: $v_0 = 0$ (starts from rest), $a = +3.0 \text{ m/s}^2$, $t = 5.0 \text{ s}$. Assume $x_0 = 0$.

Unknowns: (a) $v$, (b) $\Delta x$ (or $x$).

(a) Use $v = v_0 + at$

$v = 0 + (3.0 \text{ m/s}^2)(5.0 \text{ s}) = 15 \text{ m/s}$

(b) Use $\Delta x = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2} a t^2$

$\Delta x = (0)(5.0 \text{ s}) + \frac{1}{2} (3.0 \text{ m/s}^2) (5.0 \text{ s})^2$

$\Delta x = 0 + \frac{1}{2} (3.0 \text{ m/s}^2) (25 \text{ s}^2) = 37.5 \text{ m}$

Special Case: Free Fall

  • Motion under the influence of gravity alone (neglecting air resistance).
  • Near Earth's surface, acceleration is approximately constant and directed downwards.
  • Magnitude: $g \approx 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$.
  • Sign Convention is CRUCIAL!
    • Commonly choose "up" as positive: Then $a = -g = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$.
    • Can choose "down" as positive: Then $a = +g = +9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$.
    • Be consistent within a problem!
  • Kinematic equations apply with $a$ replaced by $+g$ or $-g$.

Example 2: Dropped Ball

A ball is dropped from rest from a height of $20.0$ m. (Neglect air resistance).

(a) How long does it take to hit the ground?

(b) What is its velocity just before impact?

Choose origin at drop point, positive direction upwards.

Knowns: $y_0 = 0$, $v_0 = 0$ (dropped from rest), $y = -20.0$ m (final position), $a = -g = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$.

Unknowns: (a) $t$, (b) $v$.

(a) Use $\Delta y = y - y_0 = v_0 t + \frac{1}{2} a t^2$

$-20.0 \text{ m} - 0 = (0)t + \frac{1}{2} (-9.8 \text{ m/s}^2) t^2$

$-20.0 = -4.9 t^2 \implies t^2 = \frac{20.0}{4.9} \approx 4.08 \text{ s}^2$

$t \approx 2.02 \text{ s}$ (Take positive root)

(b) Use $v = v_0 + at$

$v = 0 + (-9.8 \text{ m/s}^2)(2.02 \text{ s}) \approx -19.8 \text{ m/s}$

(Negative sign indicates downward velocity, which makes sense).

Alternatively use $v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\Delta y$

$v^2 = 0^2 + 2(-9.8 \text{ m/s}^2)(-20.0 \text{ m}) = 392 \text{ m}^2/\text{s}^2$

$v = \pm \sqrt{392} \approx \pm 19.8 \text{ m/s}$. Choose negative root because it's moving down.

Summary

  • Kinematics describes motion using position ($x$), velocity ($v$), and acceleration ($a$).
  • Distinguish between displacement (vector) and distance (scalar), velocity (vector) and speed (scalar).
  • Graphs ($x-t, v-t, a-t$) are powerful tools:
    • Slope of $x-t$ is $v$.
    • Slope of $v-t$ is $a$.
    • Area under $v-t$ is $\Delta x$.
    • Area under $a-t$ is $\Delta v$.
  • For constant acceleration, use the kinematic equations.
  • Free fall is a key example of constant acceleration ($a = \pm g$).
  • Follow a systematic problem-solving strategy.
